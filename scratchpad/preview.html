Chapter 1

Nuclear Spins, Net Magnetization, and the NMR Signal

From thermal polarization to excitation, relaxation, spin echoes, and the first susceptibility-sensitive intuitions for fMRI.

MRI begins with a remarkably small imbalance. Hydrogen nuclei possess magnetic moments, but at thermal equilibrium almost all microscopic contributions cancel. A strong static magnetic field creates a slight population excess in the lower-energy state, and the vector sum of that excess is the net magnetization. That tiny residual is what the scanner manipulates and detects.

ω0 = γB0     or     ν0 = γ̄B0

The resonance relation is the Larmor equation. It tells us that the precessional frequency of hydrogen is proportional to field strength. This single proportionality underlies RF tuning, slice selection, and the strong field dependence of MRI signal behavior.

RF excitation and the rotating frame

A rotating reference frame makes excitation intuitive. In the laboratory frame the transverse component of magnetization precesses rapidly. In a frame that rotates at the Larmor frequency, however, on-resonance magnetization appears stationary. A transverse RF field B1, oscillating at the Larmor frequency, then acts like an effective static field in the rotating frame and tips the net magnetization away from the z-axis.

θ = γB1Tp

The flip angle is set by the strength and duration of the RF pulse. A 90-degree pulse rotates longitudinal magnetization into the transverse plane. A 180-degree pulse inverts or refocuses, depending on context. Once magnetization is transverse, signal becomes detectable but also vulnerable to dephasing.

T2, dephasing, and spin echo logic

Immediately after excitation, spins no longer remain perfectly phase aligned. Small frequency differences caused by molecular environment and field variation cause the transverse vector sum to shrink. This decay of phase coherence is characterized by T2. Some of the dephasing is reversible, however, which is why the spin echo is so important. A 180-degree pulse swaps the positions of faster and slower phase packets so that static dephasing rewinds at the echo time.

Mxy(t) = Mxy(0)e-t/T2

T1, molecular relaxation, and tissue values

Longitudinal relaxation, or T1, describes recovery of magnetization along B0. After a 90-degree pulse, Mz begins at zero and returns toward equilibrium as energy is exchanged with the surrounding molecular environment. T2 and T1 are related but not identical. T2 is driven by phase randomization, while T1 requires energy exchange near the resonance frequency.

Mz(t) = M0(1 - e-t/T1)
Why this chapter matters
Everything later in the book depends on four facts: net magnetization is tiny but measurable, RF pulses tip it, transverse coherence decays, and some dephasing is reversible while some is not. Slice selection, k-space, EPI, BOLD contrast, dropout, and distortion all build on those facts.

Chapter Summary

MRI begins with thermally generated net magnetization in a strong static field. RF excitation tips that magnetization into the transverse plane, where it can be detected inductively as an oscillating signal. T2 describes loss of transverse coherence, T1 describes recovery of longitudinal magnetization, and spin echoes refocus reversible dephasing but not irreversible transverse relaxation. Tissue-dependent relaxation times, microscopic susceptibility gradients, and chemical shifts all foreshadow the image contrast and artifacts encountered later in MRI and fMRI.

Key Takeaways

  • Signal depends strongly on B0 because equilibrium polarization and resonance frequency both scale with field strength.
  • Only transverse magnetization produces the changing magnetic flux required for inductive detection.
  • T2 is a coherence-decay process; T1 is a longitudinal recovery process.
  • Spin echoes refocus static dephasing but not true irreversible T2 relaxation.
  • Chemical shift and vessel-scale susceptibility effects become crucial again in EPI and BOLD fMRI.

Chapter 2

MRI Scanner Hardware, Gradients, and RF Coils

How the scanner produces B0, gradients, RF excitation, and spatially structured receive sensitivity.

A scanner is best understood as a field-generating instrument rather than a mere imaging box. The main magnet creates the static field that polarizes tissue and defines resonance. The gradient system adds small spatially varying fields that make position measurable. RF hardware excites spins and receives the emitted signal. Every later topic in image formation and artifact diagnosis depends on this hardware stack.

Main field and gradients

The main magnet in a 3 T human system is usually superconducting and continuously on. It provides the large, stable B0 field needed for polarization and resonance. Gradients are comparatively small perturbations superimposed on B0, but they are the reason MRI can encode space at all. Because gradients must switch rapidly and repeatedly, their design is a compromise among strength, linearity, acoustic noise, hardware heating, and peripheral nerve stimulation.

B(x) = B0 + Gxx

Transmit and receive coils

Transmit and receive are distinct engineering jobs. The transmit coil is optimized to deliver a predictable B1 field over the imaging volume. The receive system is optimized for sensitivity and often uses many local elements rather than one large uniform detector. A phased-array receive coil uses multiple partially overlapping loops, each with its own sensitivity map, and this spatial diversity is later exploited in parallel imaging and SMS reconstruction.

Practical scanner implication
High-channel arrays are not simply “better coils.” They are more spatially structured detectors. That structure boosts SNR and enables acceleration, but it also makes receive-bias motion artifacts and reference-mismatch problems more severe.

Chapter Summary

The scanner combines four core subsystems: a strong main field, three gradient axes, RF transmission hardware, and RF reception hardware. The main field creates polarization, gradients impose spatial encoding, transmit hardware tips magnetization, and the receive system collects the signal. Modern phased-array coils improve local sensitivity and enable advanced reconstruction, but they also introduce strong spatial nonuniformity that becomes central in later discussions of motion sensitivity and bias fields.

Key Takeaways

  • MRI hardware defines the acquisition design space.
  • Gradients make spatial encoding possible and later determine distortion and echo spacing.
  • Transmit and receive are distinct physical tasks.
  • Phased-array coils improve SNR and enable acceleration.
  • Receive-field heterogeneity is an intrinsic property of array reception, not a post-processing curiosity.

Chapter 3

Fourier Transform, Gradients, and One-Dimensional MRI

Why MRI is a Fourier problem and how gradients convert position into measurable phase and frequency.

MRI does not measure an image directly. It measures a time-varying signal. The reason this signal can be turned into spatial information is that gradients impose a controlled relation between position and phase or position and frequency, and the Fourier transform is exactly the mathematical tool that inverts that encoding. Time and frequency are conjugate variables. Space and k-space are conjugate variables. MRI uses both pairs at once.

s(ν) = ∫ S(t)e-i2πνtdt

Gradients make position measurable

When a linear gradient is applied along x, the magnetic field depends on position and so does the resonance frequency. What had previously been a spatially collapsed sum of transverse magnetization becomes a superposition of position-specific phase evolutions. This is the first true spatial encoding step in MRI.

ω(x) = γ(B0 + Gxx)

The measured signal under a read gradient is therefore a Fourier transform of the spatial magnetization distribution. This is the origin of one-dimensional MRI and of the later k-space formalism. In modern notation, the coordinate in k-space is proportional to gradient area rather than to gradient amplitude alone.

kx(t) = γ̄∫Gx(τ)dτ

Chapter Summary

Fourier analysis gives MRI its decoding language. A time signal can be expressed as a set of frequency components, and a spatial image can be expressed as a set of spatial-frequency components. Gradients make spatial position measurable by turning it into phase or frequency. Under a read gradient, the acquired signal is the Fourier transform of the one-dimensional magnetization profile, and the k-space coordinate is the time integral of the gradient waveform. These ideas scale directly into full MRI image formation.

Key Takeaways

  • Time-frequency and space-k-space are the two fundamental conjugate-variable pairs in MRI.
  • Complex exponentials are the natural language of MR phase evolution.
  • A gradient maps position onto frequency or phase.
  • The measured signal under a read gradient is a spatial Fourier transform.
  • Gradient area, not gradient amplitude alone, determines k-space displacement.

Chapter 4

Slice Selection, Gradient Echoes, k-Space, and 2D Image Formation

Building a full two-dimensional MR image from selective excitation, balanced gradients, and Cartesian k-space sampling.

Once one-dimensional MRI is understood, the next challenges are obvious: excite only a chosen slice, encode a second dimension, and reconstruct a full image. The source course answers these in a logical progression: first sinc-based slice selection, then gradient-echo logic, then k-space trajectories, then line-by-line filling of a two-dimensional matrix.

Slice selection

A sinc-shaped RF pulse has an approximately rectangular spectral profile. If that pulse is played during a slice-select gradient, a narrow band of frequencies corresponds to a narrow band of z-positions. The selected frequency band therefore becomes a physical slice. Slice thickness depends on RF bandwidth divided by the frequency slope created by the gradient.

Δz = Δν / (γ̄Gz)

Gradient echoes and k-space trajectories

A gradient echo is formed by deliberately creating and then reversing gradient-induced dephasing. Unlike a spin echo, it does not use a 180-degree RF pulse and therefore does not refocus T2-prime effects from static field inhomogeneity. This is precisely why gradient echoes remain T2-star sensitive. The same sequence can be represented in two equivalent ways: as a list of gradient waveforms in time or as a path through k-space.

From image space to k-space

S(kx,ky) = ∬ I(x,y)e-i2π(kxx + kyy)dxdy

K-space is not a hidden picture of the anatomy. Each point in k-space contains information from the whole excited object, weighted by a specific spatial frequency. The center encodes broad, slowly varying structure and image contrast. The edges encode rapid spatial change and fine detail. A gradient waveform is therefore best thought of as a k-space trajectory generator.

Phase encoding and 2D Cartesian MRI

A second gradient episode along y imparts a position-dependent phase that persists after the gradient is turned off. Repeating the acquisition with different phase-encode amplitudes selects different ky lines while the readout gradient traverses kx. Once the full matrix is collected, a two-dimensional Fourier transform reconstructs the image.

Worked intuition
If the field of view is set by Δk and the nominal resolution by the maximum sampled |k|, then spatial extent and detail are controlled by different aspects of the acquisition. This is why “more matrix points” can mean several different things depending on whether one changes FOV, resolution, or both.

Chapter Summary

Selective MRI requires a band-limited RF pulse applied during a gradient so that a frequency band maps onto a slice of tissue. A slice-refocusing gradient is needed because slice selection also introduces unwanted phase dispersion. Gradient echoes form when reversible gradient-induced dephasing is unwound, but unlike spin echoes they remain sensitive to static field heterogeneity. K-space is the spatial-frequency representation of the image, and the acquisition trajectory through k-space is determined by gradient area. Conventional 2D MRI fills the k-space matrix line by line with repeated phase encodes and reconstructs the image with a 2D Fourier transform.

Key Takeaways

  • A sinc RF pulse is used for slice selection because its frequency response is approximately rectangular.
  • Slice thickness depends on RF bandwidth and slice-select gradient strength.
  • A gradient echo is not equivalent to a spin echo.
  • The center of k-space controls coarse contrast; the edges control fine detail.
  • Cartesian 2D MRI is built from repeated slice selection, phase encoding, and readout.

Basic MRI Sampling Artifacts and Operational Constraints

Aliasing, truncation artifact, and stimulation limits as early examples of the tradeoffs built into MRI sampling.

Aliasing and Gibbs ringing are not arbitrary nuisances. They are direct manifestations of MRI’s Fourier structure. Aliasing reflects insufficient field of view for the object extent, whereas truncation artifact reflects insufficient k-space extent for a sharp edge. Gradient-stimulation limits add a second lesson: MRI is not only a mathematical sampling problem but also a physiological one.

Artifact recognition
Wrap-around looks like real anatomy folded into the wrong place. Gibbs ringing looks like alternating bright and dark bands near a sharp boundary. Distinguishing these two failures early makes later EPI troubleshooting much easier.

Chapter Summary

Aliasing occurs when the reconstructed field of view is smaller than the anatomical extent of the object, so repeated copies of the image overlap. Truncation artifact, or Gibbs ringing, occurs when k-space is sampled over only a finite extent and sharp edges are therefore represented by oscillatory side lobes. Gradient switching is limited by induced electric fields in tissue, so the fastest mathematically attractive acquisition is not always physiologically permissible.

Key Takeaways

  • Aliasing is a field-of-view problem caused by too-large Δk.
  • Gibbs ringing is a finite-k-space-extent problem caused by missing high spatial frequencies.
  • The appearance of an artifact often reveals which part of the sampling design failed.
  • Gradient slew rate matters because switching fields induce electric fields in tissue.
  • Sampling efficiency must always be balanced against hardware and physiology.

Key Terms

  • Wrap-around
  • Gibbs ringing

Chapter 6

Echo-Planar Imaging and the Three Classic EPI Artifacts

Why EPI is fast, why GRE EPI is fragile, and how ghosting, distortion, and dropout arise.

EPI accelerates MRI by collecting many lines of k-space after a single excitation. The reward is speed sufficient for whole-brain fMRI. The cost is an extended gradient-echo readout during which timing mismatch, off-resonance, T2-star decay, and susceptibility gradients all continue to act. The classic trio of EPI artifacts - Nyquist ghosting, distortion, and dropout - are all consequences of that design choice.

Nyquist ghosting

In EPI, alternate k-space lines are acquired with opposite read-gradient polarity. If the sampling clock, gradient timing, or phase evolution differs slightly between odd and even echoes, adjacent lines no longer align as mirror versions of one another. After time reversal of alternate lines, the mismatch appears as a line-to-line zigzag in k-space. In image space the result is the classic Nyquist ghost, typically displaced by half the field of view along the phase-encode direction.

Chemical shift, fat suppression, and ramp sampling

Fat is especially troublesome in EPI because the water-fat chemical shift, modest in hertz, becomes large in pixels along the phase-encode direction where the bandwidth per pixel is extremely low. Bright scalp fat can therefore be displaced dramatically and can drive strong ghosts if not suppressed. Ramp sampling pushes speed further by collecting signal on the rising and falling edges of the read gradient, but if the gradient waveform or timing is not executed faithfully the ghosting burden can increase rather than decrease.

Distortion and dropout

ΔpPE = Δfoff / BWPE

Distortion in EPI arises because the phase-encode bandwidth per pixel is tiny compared with the readout bandwidth. Local off-resonance from susceptibility therefore displaces signal by several pixels along the phase-encode axis. Dropout is different: signal is not moved but lost because spins within a voxel or slice dephase across local field gradients. Thinner slices reduce through-slice dephasing, whereas shorter TE reduces the time available for that dephasing to accumulate.

Slice order and real product EPI

Multi-slice EPI introduces a further complication: slice order and spin history. If the subject moves, tissue can enter a slice with a different recent excitation history than the tissue originally occupying that location, and the resulting signal fluctuation cannot be repaired by rigid-body realignment alone. Real product EPI sequences therefore combine fat saturation, selective excitation, crushers, ghost-correction echoes, and the main gradient-echo train into a tightly choreographed whole.

Chapter Summary

EPI is a multi-gradient-echo sequence that collects many k-space lines after one excitation. Its speed makes fMRI practical, but its extended readout creates three classic problems. Nyquist ghosting arises from odd-even mismatch and usually appears at FOV/2 in the phase direction. Distortion arises because low phase-encode bandwidth converts modest off-resonance into large pixel shifts. Dropout arises from intravoxel and through-slice dephasing in susceptibility gradients. Chemical shift makes scalp fat especially dangerous in EPI, ramp sampling must be balanced against timing fidelity, and slice order can create spin-history instability in moving subjects. Real product EPI sequences explicitly contain ghost-correction and spoiling steps because these failure modes are fundamental, not accidental.

Key Takeaways

  • EPI trades repeated excitations for repeated echoes.
  • Nyquist ghosts come from odd-even inconsistency, not just from “noise.”
  • Fat suppression is especially important in EPI because chemical-shift displacement is large in the phase direction.
  • Distortion and dropout are distinct even though they often occur together.
  • Good EPI is judged by stability and interpretability, not by a fantasy of perfect images.

Chapter 7

Susceptibility, T2-star, Motion, Inflow, and Receive-Field Confounds

How the same physics that creates BOLD contrast also creates geometric error, intensity instability, and interpretive ambiguity.

The brain is never perfectly still, the magnetic field is never perfectly homogeneous, and GRE EPI is sensitive to both facts. This chapter traces the shared physical roots of BOLD sensitivity and BOLD confounding: susceptibility-induced field gradients, T2-star decay during the readout, physiological inflow effects, and receive-field nonuniformity interacting with motion correction.

Susceptibility as both signal source and artifact source

Magnetic susceptibility describes how a material perturbs the applied field. Variations in susceptibility create intrinsic local gradients that the scanner cannot turn off. Around the paranasal sinuses, ear canals, and other air-tissue interfaces, these gradients are strong enough to warp anatomy and erase signal. Around blood vessels, however, subtler susceptibility changes generate the T2-star sensitivity exploited by BOLD fMRI. The same category of physics therefore underlies both the desired contrast and the undesired artifact burden.

Flip angle, inflow, and temporal SNR

Flowing blood does not share the same recent excitation history as stationary tissue. When fresh blood enters the slice, it can be less saturated than the tissue already inside, which gives it an effectively shorter apparent T1 in the imaging context. Higher flip angles strengthen this inflow weighting. They can slightly increase measured functional signal and can shift the apparent response earlier in time, but they do not necessarily improve temporal stability. In fMRI, the flip angle that maximizes image brightness is often not the flip angle that maximizes temporal SNR.

tSNR = mean signal / temporal standard deviation

Receive-bias motion and anchoring

Receive arrays detect tissue through steeply varying sensitivity fields. If the head moves through that field, the same anatomical structure can be multiplied by different receive gains at different time points. Rigid-body realignment can restore geometry, but it cannot retroactively remove the multiplicative intensity modulation already baked into the data. This receive-field-contrast motion-correction interaction, often abbreviated RFC-MoCo, is one of the most important modern fMRI confounds.

Practical scanner implication
High-channel coils boost SNR and support acceleration, but the gain is coupled to a sharper receive field. Motion control, prescan normalization, and cautious interpretation become more important, not less, as receive arrays become more structured.

Chapter Summary

Susceptibility differences create intrinsic local field gradients. Around air-tissue interfaces these gradients drive distortion and dropout, while around vessels they create the T2-star sensitivity exploited by BOLD fMRI. T2-star decay continues during the EPI readout itself, which is why TE must be chosen as a contrast tradeoff rather than as a purely geometric choice. Flowing blood introduces inflow and spin-history effects that depend on flip angle and can alter both amplitude and timing of the measured response. Mean signal and temporal SNR are not optimized by the same flip angle. Finally, receive-field heterogeneity makes motion an intensity-modulation problem as well as a geometric one, and the resulting RFC-MoCo effect can survive perfect realignment.

Key Takeaways

  • Susceptibility is both the basis of BOLD contrast and the basis of several major EPI artifacts.
  • Phase maps are practical diagnostics for off-resonance structure.
  • High flip angles can increase inflow weighting without improving temporal SNR.
  • Motion through a receive-bias field creates real signal modulation that rigid-body correction cannot undo.
  • Prescan normalization can reduce receive-field dominance during volume realignment.

Chapter 8

Partial Fourier EPI

How asymmetric k-space sampling shortens the EPI train, what it buys, and what it costs.

Partial Fourier is one of the simplest ways to accelerate EPI. Instead of measuring the full k-space matrix, one side of k-space is omitted and later reconstructed approximately. The motivation is that k-space contains redundancy: if image phase is constrained, one side partially predicts the other. But the omitted data are still real missing measurements, so acceleration is obtained at the cost of SNR, point-spread broadening, and anatomy-dependent vulnerability in susceptibility-prone regions.

Early versus late omission

Because EPI is ordered in time, omitting early echoes is not equivalent to omitting late echoes. Early omission shortens TE, which can reduce BOLD sensitivity even while improving geometric robustness. Late omission leaves the echo center closer to the desired T2-star weighting while shortening the tail of the train, often allowing greater slice coverage within a fixed TR. In BOLD-oriented GRE EPI, late omission is therefore often preferable when the implementation allows it.

Dropout tradeoffs, phase-encode direction, and smoothing

Partial Fourier can worsen dropout because real tissue in susceptibility gradients does not follow the idealized k-space trajectory perfectly. Frontal and temporal regions are especially vulnerable. Which regions degrade most depends on whether early or late echoes are missing and on the chosen phase-encode direction. Missing data also broaden the effective point-spread function, which produces mild smoothing even before any explicit post-processing.

Chapter Summary

Partial Fourier EPI shortens the readout by omitting one side of k-space and reconstructing the missing portion approximately. Its conceptual basis is conjugate symmetry, but real image phase means that the symmetry is never exact. The most important practical choice is whether to omit early or late echoes. Early omission shortens TE; late omission often preserves a BOLD-appropriate TE while increasing slice coverage more effectively. Partial Fourier can worsen dropout in susceptibility-prone regions and introduces implicit smoothing because incomplete sampling broadens the point-spread function. Its main advantage is that it does not rely heavily on separate reference data and therefore adds less motion sensitivity than GRAPPA or SMS.

Key Takeaways

  • Partial Fourier saves time by measuring less k-space, not by measuring the same data more efficiently.
  • Early and late omission are physically different because EPI has a temporal order.
  • Late omission is often preferable in GRE BOLD EPI because it preserves TE.
  • Partial Fourier can change dropout patterns in anatomy-dependent ways.
  • Mild smoothing is built into the method because the point-spread function broadens.

In-Plane Acceleration and GRAPPA

Parallel imaging as deliberate undersampling plus coil-based reconstruction.

GRAPPA shortens the EPI train by skipping regularly spaced phase-encode lines and using phased-array coil information to reconstruct what was not measured. Without reconstruction, the image would simply fold over in the phase direction. The reason GRAPPA works is that different coil elements observe the head with different sensitivity patterns, so aliased locations contribute differently across channels. That spatial diversity supplies the missing information.

ACS dependence and motion sensitivity

The ACS, or autocalibration signal, is the reference dataset from which GRAPPA learns how to synthesize missing lines. This creates a failure mode absent from plain EPI and largely absent from partial Fourier. Motion during ACS corrupts the reference itself. Motion after ACS can leave a persistent mismatch between the calibration and the current subject position. Even if the participant later becomes still, the reconstruction can remain suboptimal because the coil-sensitivity geometry encoded by the ACS no longer matches the time series.

SNRR ≈ SNR0 / (√R · g)
Tradeoff summary
GRAPPA reduces distortion by shortening the phase-encode train, but it pays for that reduction with fewer measured data, geometry-factor penalties, and much stronger sensitivity to motion relative to the ACS reference.

Chapter Summary

GRAPPA is a parallel-imaging method that accelerates EPI by undersampling the phase-encode direction and reconstructing missing lines from a phased-array coil reference dataset. Its main geometric benefit is a shorter train and therefore less phase-encode distortion. Its main liabilities are SNR loss and major motion sensitivity because the reconstruction depends on ACS data. Motion during ACS corrupts the calibration itself; motion after ACS mismatches the time series to the stored calibration. GRAPPA is therefore powerful but unforgiving.

Key Takeaways

  • GRAPPA converts receive-coil diversity into spatial encoding information.
  • Undersampling without reconstruction would simply fold the image in the phase direction.
  • ACS data are essential because they teach the missing-line synthesis weights.
  • GRAPPA reduces distortion but not for free; SNR and calibration robustness both matter.
  • Motion relative to ACS can damage an entire time series even when later frames look visually reasonable.

Chapter 10

SMS-EPI, Multi-Echo EPI, and FLEET

Slice acceleration, echo-time diversity, and smarter reference acquisition.

Modern fMRI protocols often combine several advanced ideas at once: simultaneous multi-slice excitation to shorten TR, in-plane acceleration to shorten the readout, multi-echo acquisition to gain TE information, and special reference strategies to protect calibration from motion. These methods are powerful precisely because they exploit information outside the simple single-shot picture - coil sensitivity along the slice axis, echo-time dependence of contrast, and careful ordering of reference data.

SMS and controlled aliasing

Simultaneous multi-slice imaging, also called multiband imaging, excites several slices at once by summing multiple slice-selective RF bands. Separation afterward depends on the receive array: different slices project into different coil-sensitivity patterns, especially when the array has substantial structure along the superior-inferior direction. Blipped-CAIPI deliberately shifts the aliased slice images relative to one another in order to make the unaliasing problem better conditioned.

Multi-echo EPI

Multi-echo EPI acquires two or more echoes after each excitation. The signal therefore samples the same anatomy at different echo times, which can be used in two distinct ways. First, echoes can be combined to improve regional robustness, especially in regions where short echoes preserve signal that long echoes lose. Second, TE dependence can be used to classify signal changes. A true R2-star-like BOLD change scales with echo time, whereas a purely S0-like amplitude fluctuation is approximately TE independent in fractional terms.

S(TE) = S0e-TE·R2*

FLEET reference acquisition

FLEET, or Fast Low-angle Excitation Echo-planar Technique, is best understood as a reference-acquisition strategy rather than a new contrast mechanism. Instead of spacing ACS segments for a given slice far apart in time, FLEET acquires them back-to-back at low flip angle. The result is a calibration dataset that is less vulnerable to motion and spin-history inconsistency during the reference itself.

Chapter Summary

SMS accelerates the slice dimension by exciting multiple slices at once and separating them using coil sensitivity differences and reference data. Blipped-CAIPI improves that separation by shaping the aliasing pattern deliberately. SMS can dramatically shorten TR, but it inherits strong calibration sensitivity from its single-band reference and practical limits from SNR and total acceleration burden. Multi-echo EPI adds TE diversity, which can improve regional signal via weighted combination and can separate TE-dependent from TE-independent fluctuations. FLEET improves the robustness of reference acquisition by collecting segments for a given slice back-to-back with low flip angle.

Key Takeaways

  • SMS requires informative coil sensitivity along the slice axis.
  • Controlled aliasing can make an otherwise fragile reconstruction much more stable.
  • Multi-echo methods help because BOLD contrast has a characteristic TE dependence.
  • BOLD-like is not the same as neural.
  • FLEET is a calibration strategy designed to reduce reference inconsistency.

Chapter 11

Artifact Recognition, QC, and Troubleshooting

How to tell good EPI from bad EPI, and how to diagnose motion, hardware, and reconstruction failures systematically.

Quality control in fMRI is not an administrative afterthought. It is part of the measurement itself. A dataset can have an acceptable mean image and still fail as a time series because of fluctuating ghosts, residual aliasing, receive-bias modulation, or hardware drift. The correct diagnostic mindset therefore begins with comparison: know what good data look like for the sequence, know what normal residual artifacts look like, and then determine which deviations are large enough or unstable enough to threaten interpretation.

Normal artifact patterns

Weak Nyquist ghosting is normal in many EPI datasets, especially in the background. What matters is where the ghost falls and whether it changes over time. Scalp ghosts and eye-movement ghosts are especially important because they can overlap ventral frontal or temporal regions of interest. Temporal standard-deviation maps and tSNR maps are often more informative than the mean image because they show where instability lives rather than merely where anatomy is bright.

Residual aliasing and movement classes

Accelerated methods introduce their own recognizable failure patterns. Residual GRAPPA aliasing does not look like a simple FOV/2 Nyquist ghost. SMS leakage does not look like ordinary distortion. At the same time, movement is not a single artifact class. Eye movements, head movements, talking, and even foot motion can all drive structured EPI variance through different mechanical and physiological pathways.

Coil motion, structural motion, and hardware faults

Not all apparent motion is subject motion. Receive-coil housings can have mechanical play, and if the coil moves relative to the head the receive field moves too. Structural scans also need dedicated QC because motion there compromises registration and downstream anatomy-based analysis. Finally, foreign objects, RF interference, gradient spiking, RF-coil spikes, and slow drift all have characteristic patterns that can often be separated from physiology by retest, cross-sequence comparison, or phantom scanning.

Diagnostic workflow
  1. Assess temporal stability, not just the mean image.
  2. Acquire a short retest when feasible.
  3. List a small number of plausible explanations.
  4. Ask whether the problem appears in a different scan type or phantom.
  5. Consider re-setup of the subject and coil.

Chapter Summary

Artifact recognition begins with knowing what normal data look like. Weak ghosts, susceptibility effects, and receive bias are expected; the important questions are where they fall and whether they vary across time. tSNR and temporal-standard-deviation images are indispensable because fMRI is fundamentally a time-series measurement. Residual aliasing from GRAPPA or SMS has a different geometry from ordinary Nyquist ghosts, and motion has multiple pathways into the data, including the eyes, speech, limbs, the coil, and the hardware. Structural motion, foreign objects, RF interference, gradient spiking, and slow drift all leave distinctive signatures. The most reliable troubleshooting is comparative, targeted, and hypothesis driven.

Key Takeaways

  • A good EPI image is not artifact free; it is stable and interpretable.
  • Temporal QC is often more important than single-frame QC in fMRI.
  • Residual aliasing from accelerated methods has its own appearance and must be learned.
  • Apparent motion may originate in the coil, bed, or hardware, not just the head.
  • Phantom retests and cross-sequence comparisons are powerful diagnostic tools.

Chapter 12

Biological Confounds and Human Modifiers in fMRI

A systems view of neural, vascular, autonomic, respiratory, and human-state pathways into the final time series.

The final fMRI dataset is not produced by neural activity alone. It is generated by a coupled biological-and-measurement system in which brain activity, autonomic state, respiration, blood gases, vascular anatomy, motion, scanner stability, and subject state all interact. The most important practical message of the source course is that the relative importance of these pathways depends on study design. A nuisance that is moderate in a short task study may be critical in a resting-state, longitudinal, pharmacologic, or patient-group study.

Design-specific confound importance

The source deck provides several ranking tables precisely because there is no universal confound hierarchy. Task designs, naturalistic paradigms, resting-state studies, pharmacologic interventions, longitudinal protocols, and patient-group comparisons each emphasize different vulnerabilities. Resting-state and intervention studies are especially exposed to physiology and arousal. Longitudinal and patient-group studies are especially exposed to anything that differs systematically across visits or populations.

Human modifiers and auxiliary measurements

Human factors such as hormones, caffeine, smoking, medications, recent exercise, sleep history, hematocrit, body size, and inspired gases are not mere demographic annotations. They are mechanistic modifiers of the measurement chain. Likewise, simultaneous physiological monitoring and small supporting MRI acquisitions can materially improve interpretability because they reveal whether a measured fluctuation is neural, vascular, autonomic, or mixed.

Study-design guidance
Task fMRI benefits from strong timing structure but still requires motion and state control. Resting-state studies need physiology monitoring and subject-state control most urgently. Pharmacologic, longitudinal, and patient-group studies demand especially careful attention to variables that change systematically across sessions or groups.

Chapter Summary

fMRI is produced by a coupled system linking neural activity, autonomic physiology, respiration, heart rate, vascular state, motion, and scanner behavior. The importance of any given confound depends on study design. Human factors such as caffeine, hormones, sleep, smoking, medications, body composition, and inspired gases are mechanistic modifiers of the measurement, not background trivia. Auxiliary physiological monitoring and supporting MRI scans can substantially improve interpretation. The core operational lesson is that better confound control begins before scanning, with protocol design and deliberate measurement of the variables most likely to matter for the scientific question.

Key Takeaways

  • Confounds should be understood as mechanisms, not merely nuisance regressors.
  • There is no universal ranking of confounds across all fMRI study types.
  • Human-state variables such as caffeine and sleep can alter both physiology and interpretation.
  • Auxiliary measurements can materially improve scientific specificity.
  • Good fMRI study design begins before the participant enters the scanner.

Appendix B. Practical Troubleshooting Checklists

Ghosting checklist
  • Check phase-encode direction and FOV/2 displacement.
  • Check fat suppression and scalp signal.
  • Inspect temporal variance, not just one frame.
  • Consider ramp-sampling aggressiveness and ghost-correction reference quality.
Distortion checklist
  • Inspect shimming quality and field-map logic.
  • Check phase-encode polarity relative to the region of interest.
  • Reduce echo spacing when feasible.
  • Distinguish geometric misplacement from true signal loss.
Dropout checklist
  • Check TE and slice thickness.
  • Inspect orbitofrontal and temporal anatomy carefully.
  • Consider thinner slices or altered coverage geometry.
  • Remember that GRAPPA or partial Fourier can change appearance without truly restoring lost coherence.
Acceleration checklist
  • For GRAPPA, ask what happened during and after ACS.
  • For SMS, ask what happened during and after SBRef.
  • Look for residual aliasing geometry, not just “noise.”
  • Treat high R × SMS combinations cautiously in moving populations.

Glossary

ACS
Autocalibration data used to train GRAPPA reconstruction.
Aliasing
Misregistration caused by undersampling, often seen as wrap-around or fold-over.
BOLD
Blood-oxygen-level-dependent contrast, usually measured with GRE EPI.
Conjugate symmetry
Hermitian relation in k-space that permits partial Fourier reconstruction under phase constraints.
Distortion
Geometric misplacement of signal, especially along the phase-encode direction in EPI.
Dropout
Signal loss caused by destructive intravoxel or through-slice dephasing.
EPI
Echo-planar imaging, a rapid sequence that acquires many k-space lines after one excitation.
FLEET
Fast Low-angle Excitation Echo-planar Technique for more robust ACS acquisition.
GRAPPA
Parallel-imaging method that reconstructs missing k-space lines from coil reference data.
Inflow effect
Signal contribution from fresh spins entering the imaging slice with less saturation.
k-space
The spatial-frequency domain sampled in MRI.
Nyquist ghost
The classic FOV/2 EPI ghost arising from odd-even echo mismatch.
RFC-MoCo
Receive-field-contrast motion-correction interaction that leaves residual intensity modulation after realignment.
SMS
Simultaneous multi-slice imaging, also called multiband imaging.
tSNR
Temporal SNR, the ratio of mean signal to temporal standard deviation over time.
T2-star
Effective transverse decay including both true T2 processes and static-field-inhomogeneity effects.
